# **Operational Model**

## **League of Tech Mentors**

## **Overview**

The League of Tech Mentors functions as a distribution network
connecting national curriculum developers with local
communities---similar to how a regional beverage distributor connects
manufacturers like Coca-Cola with local restaurants and retailers. We
don\'t create the product (curriculum) or consume it (students), but we
provide the critical infrastructure that ensures reliable, consistent
delivery at the local level. By centralizing operational functions that
currently require each organization to build independently---promotional
systems, instructor pipelines, venue relationships, administrative
infrastructure---we dramatically reduce the cost per student served
while improving quality and continuity.

Our hub-and-spoke model operates through six interconnected functions
that solve the coordination challenges outlined in our problem
statement:

• **National Curriculum Partnerships** - Establish agreements with
organizations like Code.org, Girls Who Code, Raspberry Pi Foundation,
and Black Girls Code to deploy their curricula locally with trained
instructors

• **Venue Development and Site Relationships** - Build ongoing
partnerships with schools, libraries, recreation centers, and community
organizations that host programming events

• **Local Delivery Partner Network** - Contract with existing coding
schools, STEM nonprofits, and individual instructors to provide
qualified instruction for community programs

• **Marketing and Registration Infrastructure** - Provide centralized
promotional systems (PeachJar, social media, flyers) and registration
platforms that local providers cannot access independently

• **Volunteer Management System** - Recruit, train, vet, and deploy
volunteers who support program delivery while creating a talent pipeline
for local providers

• **Regional Gear Libraries** - Maintain equipment collections
(computers, robotics kits, microcontrollers) available for loan to
programs, schools, and community organizations

## **1. National Curriculum Partnerships**

### **Purpose and Value Proposition**

National organizations invest millions in developing high-quality CS
education curricula but struggle to deploy these materials consistently
at the local level. We solve their \"last mile\" problem by providing
professional infrastructure for sustained delivery across multiple
metropolitan areas. Rather than each organization building separate
\$20,000-60,000 per year regional operations, they partner with our
network to reach communities at a fraction of the cost.

### **Partnership Development Process**

**Initial Engagement:** We approach national organizations with a clear
value proposition: their curricula will reach more students, in more
communities, with better continuity and measurable outcomes than they
can achieve through volunteer-only models. Our track record in San Diego
(10+ years, 500+ students annually) provides proof of concept.

**Curriculum Access and Training:** Partner organizations provide their
curricula, training materials, and brand guidelines. We train our
regional coordinators and delivery partners on proper implementation,
ensuring fidelity to the original program design while adapting for
after-school and library settings versus classroom instruction.

**Deployment Agreements:** We establish formal partnerships defining:

-   Geographic scope (which metros we\'ll deploy their programs)

-   Program formats (one-hour introductions, multi-week series, ongoing
    > clubs)

-   Quality standards and instructor qualifications

-   Co-branding and marketing guidelines

-   Reporting requirements (demographics, outcomes, student progression)

-   Cost sharing for regional coordinator expenses

**Ongoing Coordination:** Regular communication ensures alignment as
curricula evolve, new materials become available, and we expand to
additional markets. Partners receive detailed reports on student
participation, demographics, and program outcomes that they can share
with their own funders.

### **Target National Partners**

**Primary Partners:**

-   Code.org (CS fundamentals, Hour of Code events)

-   Girls Who Code (Clubs program, Summer Immersion)

-   Black Girls Code (workshops focused on girls of color)

-   Raspberry Pi Foundation (physical computing, creative technology)

-   Grant Imahara Foundation (robotics education)

-   Hack Club (student-led coding clubs)

**Secondary Partners:**

-   AI4K12.org (emerging AI education curricula)

-   CSTA (Computer Science Teachers Association - professional
    > development)

-   State and regional CS education initiatives

### **Financial Model**

Partners contribute to regional coordinator costs proportional to the
volume of their programming we deploy. A typical arrangement might be:

-   Partner pays \$15,000-25,000 annually per metro for deployment
    > rights

-   This covers their share of regional coordinator salary and
    > operations

-   Compares favorably to \$80,000-100,000 to build independent presence

-   Multiple partners sharing one coordinator reduces per-org cost by
    > 70-80%

## **2. Venue Development and Site Relationships**

### **Building the Venue Network**

Sustained programming requires reliable access to facilities where
students naturally congregate. Regional coordinators build ongoing
relationships with venue administrators, understanding their space
availability, target audiences, scheduling constraints, and programming
interests.

**Public Libraries:** Libraries actively seek free educational
programming for children and teens. Most metro areas have 15-30+ branch
locations, providing geographic distribution that reaches diverse
neighborhoods. Libraries offer:

-   Free space for community programs

-   Built-in promotion through library newsletters and calendars

-   Access to families already seeking educational resources

-   Equipment (sometimes) including computers, WiFi, projectors

-   Established safety protocols and liability coverage

**Public Schools:** After-school programs at schools serve students who
already attend, eliminating transportation barriers. Schools provide:

-   Consistent student populations over time

-   Computer labs and necessary equipment

-   Connection to teachers who can identify interested students

-   Potential pathways to in-school CS education

-   Access to Title I funding for underserved communities

**Recreation Centers and Community Organizations:** Rec centers, Boys
and Girls Clubs, YMCA/YWCA locations, and similar organizations offer:

-   Regular programming schedules seeking educational content

-   Diverse student populations including underserved communities

-   Facilities designed for youth activities

-   Staff experienced in managing children\'s programs

-   Often serve students during summer when schools are closed

**Private Schools and Charter Schools:** Some private institutions want
CS education but lack expertise or resources to build programs
internally. They may contract for our services or provide space for free
community programs.

### **Relationship Management**

**Initial Outreach:** Regional coordinators introduce the program to
venue administrators, emphasizing:

-   Free programming that serves their community mission

-   Minimal burden on their staff (we handle promotion, registration,
    > instruction)

-   Flexible scheduling that fits their calendar

-   Quality instruction from vetted, trained instructors

-   Measurable outcomes they can report to their stakeholders

**Memoranda of Understanding (MOUs):** Formal agreements establish
expectations:

-   Space reservation procedures and access protocols

-   Equipment availability and responsibilities

-   Safety and emergency procedures

-   Insurance and liability coverage

-   Promotion and marketing guidelines

-   Schedule coordination and cancellation policies

-   Conflict resolution and termination provisions

**Ongoing Coordination:** Regular communication maintains relationships:

-   Quarterly planning meetings to schedule upcoming programs

-   Post-program evaluations and feedback collection

-   Adjustments based on venue needs and student response

-   Recognition of venue contributions (annual appreciation events)

-   Problem-solving when challenges arise

### **Geographic Strategy**

Regional coordinators prioritize venue development to maximize reach
while managing operational complexity:

-   **Year 1:** Establish 5-8 core venues with consistent programming

-   **Year 2:** Expand to 12-15 venues, adding geographic diversity

-   **Year 3:** Reach 20+ venues achieving metro-wide coverage

-   **Priority:** Title I schools and underserved communities align with
    > funder priorities

## **3. Local Delivery Partner Network**

### **The Provider Partnership Model**

Rather than hiring our own instructional staff in every community, we
contract with existing local providers who already have qualified
instructors, operational infrastructure, and community presence. This
approach:

-   Leverages existing capacity rather than building from scratch

-   Supports local businesses and nonprofits financially

-   Provides quality instruction from experienced educators

-   Scales efficiently as we expand to new metros

-   Creates sustainability even if our funding fluctuates

### **Types of Delivery Partners**

**For-Profit Coding Schools:** Code Ninjas, Coder School, iCode, and
independent coding schools struggle with customer acquisition costs and
cannot access nonprofit promotional channels. We offer them:

-   Contracted revenue for delivering free community programs (\$250 per
    > one-off session, \$150 setup + instructor costs for ongoing
    > programs)

-   Access to PeachJar and other nonprofit-only promotional channels

-   A talent pipeline through our volunteer program (solving their #1
    > operational challenge)

-   Community credibility through association with national partners

-   Geographic territory protection (respecting franchise agreements)

**Local STEM Nonprofits:** Organizations with educational missions but
limited capacity benefit from:

-   Contract to employ our regional coordinator (steady operational
    > funding)

-   Connection to national funding sources

-   Shared curriculum and training resources

-   Equipment library access and coordination

-   Professional development for their staff

**Independent Instructors:** Individual educators with technical skills
can:

-   Receive training on our curricula and teaching methods

-   Earn \$30-40/hour as contractors for program delivery

-   Build their professional portfolio and credentials

-   Connect with coding schools seeking to hire instructors

-   Maintain flexible schedules around other employment

### **Partnership Agreements**

**Standard Contracts Define:**

-   Scope of services (which programs, how many sessions)

-   Instructor qualifications and vetting requirements

-   Compensation rates and payment terms

-   Quality standards and evaluation processes

-   Use of curricula and co-branding guidelines

-   Liability and insurance provisions

-   Term and renewal conditions

**Quality Assurance:** We maintain program quality through:

-   Pre-approval of instructors based on qualifications

-   Observation of delivery partners during programs

-   Student and parent feedback collection

-   Ongoing training and professional development

-   Clear performance standards and improvement protocols

-   Ability to discontinue partnerships for quality concerns

### **Geographic Coverage**

In a typical metro area, we might work with:

-   2-3 established coding schools as primary delivery partners

-   1-2 local STEM nonprofits for specialized programs

-   5-8 individual contractor instructors for flexible capacity

-   1-2 volunteer-led programs for community-owned sustainability

This creates redundancy so programs aren\'t dependent on any single
provider, while giving us flexibility to scale up during high-demand
periods (summer) and maintain core programming during slower seasons.

## **4. Marketing and Registration Infrastructure**

### **The Promotional Advantage**

One of our most valuable services to both national partners and local
providers is access to promotional channels that for-profit
organizations cannot use and individual nonprofits struggle to leverage
effectively.

**PeachJar Distribution:** PeachJar (and similar school communication
platforms) allows electronic flyer distribution to all families in
participating school districts---but only for 501(c)(3) nonprofits. As
the nonprofit front, we provide:

-   District-wide reach (San Diego Unified: 120,000 students, LAUSD:
    > 600,000 students)

-   Co-branded flyers featuring our organization, national partners, and
    > local delivery sites

-   Professional graphic design and copywriting

-   Coordination of distribution timing and frequency

-   Cost coverage (\$300-500 per distribution)

**School and Library Newsletters:** Venues promote programs through
their existing communication channels:

-   Library newsletters and website event calendars

-   School principal newsletters and parent bulletins

-   Community organization email lists and social media

-   Recreation center program guides and seasonal catalogs

**Digital Marketing:** We maintain professional online presence:

-   Website with program calendar and registration system

-   Facebook events and targeted social media promotion

-   Google Calendar integration with community STEM event listings

-   Partnership with local news outlets for calendar submissions

-   Email marketing to families who\'ve participated previously

**Community Outreach:** Regional coordinators build visibility through:

-   Presentations at local coding schools about free community programs

-   Tabling at community events, maker faires, and STEM festivals

-   Partnership with museums and science centers for cross-promotion

-   Press releases to local media about program launches and milestones

### **Registration and Administration**

**Pike 13 Platform:** We use Pike 13 (or similar registration software)
to handle:

-   Event listings with descriptions, dates, times, locations

-   Online registration with capacity limits

-   Automated confirmation emails and reminders

-   Attendance tracking during programs

-   Payment processing (for any fee-based programs)

-   Parent communication tools

-   Data collection for reporting (demographics, outcomes)

**Operational Efficiency:** Centralized registration means:

-   Delivery partners don\'t need their own registration systems

-   Consistent data collection across all programs

-   Professional parent experience regardless of venue

-   Easy reporting to funders and national partners

-   Reduced administrative burden on volunteers and instructors

### **Co-Branding Strategy**

All promotional materials feature:

-   Our organization logo and mission

-   National partner logo (Code.org, Girls Who Code, etc.)

-   Local delivery partner acknowledgment

-   Venue/location information

-   Funder recognition when appropriate

This co-branding provides credibility for local providers, visibility
for national partners, and donor recognition---while maintaining our
role as the coordinating organization.

## **5. Volunteer Management System**

### **The Strategic Role of Volunteers**

Volunteers serve three critical functions in our operational model:

1.  **Cost Reduction:** Volunteer instructional hours reduce program
    > delivery costs by 60-70% compared to paid-only instruction

2.  **Talent Pipeline:** The volunteer program solves local coding
    > schools\' #1 challenge by providing a \"working interview\" system
    > that identifies candidates with both technical skills and teaching
    > ability

3.  **Community Ownership:** Trained volunteers can eventually lead
    > independent clubs, creating sustainability beyond our direct
    > involvement

### **Three-Tier Volunteer Pathway**

**Tier 1: Event Volunteers** provide one-off support for free Tech Club
sessions, helping paid instructors with student support, setup, and
demonstrations. Volunteers at this tier:

-   Commit to individual events (2-3 hours each) with no ongoing
    > obligation

-   Receive basic orientation and training (3 hours)

-   Work under direct supervision of paid lead instructors

-   Build teaching experience with low commitment

-   Can evaluate if they want deeper involvement

**Tier 2: Program Instructors** commit to delivering complete 6-10 week
program series, requiring:

-   Consistent weekly availability over program duration

-   Ability to lead sessions independently

-   Deeper curriculum knowledge and teaching skills

-   Additional training (6 hours total) beyond Tier 1

-   May receive stipends (\$20-30/hour) or work as contractors
    > (\$30-40/hour)

**Tier 3: Community Club Leaders** operate clubs independently at their
own organizations (schools, scout troops, community centers):

-   Receive specialized training (4 hours) for running clubs

-   Access League curriculum library and technical support

-   Background checked by their own organization

-   Create sustainability beyond our direct involvement

-   Enable programming in locations we don\'t directly serve

### **Recruitment Strategies**

**College Students:** Computer Science, Education, and Engineering
majors benefit from:

-   Resume-building teaching experience

-   Practical application of technical skills

-   Professional networking opportunities

-   Service learning credit at some universities

**Tech Professionals:** Software engineers and IT professionals seeking
to:

-   Give back to their communities

-   Explore teaching as potential career change

-   Develop coaching and mentoring skills

-   Break from screen time with hands-on work with children

**Career Changers:** Individuals considering transitions to teaching or
tech:

-   Test whether they enjoy teaching technology to children

-   Build credentials and experience

-   Make professional connections

-   Low-commitment exploration of new career paths

**Retired Professionals:** Former engineers and educators who want to:

-   Stay engaged and mentor the next generation

-   Share accumulated expertise

-   Maintain flexible volunteer schedules

-   Contribute to meaningful community impact

### **Training and Vetting**

**Orientation Process:**

1.  Initial webinar (60 minutes) explaining program mission and
    > volunteer roles

2.  Observation at first event (no background check required, fully
    > supervised)

3.  Decision point: volunteer and staff both decide if good fit

4.  If continuing: volunteer training workshop (3 hours)

5.  Background check and mandatory reporter training

6.  Assignment to regular events as Tier 1 volunteer

**Training Curriculum Covers:**

-   Working with young students (child development, learning styles)

-   Technical support strategies (helping without solving for students)

-   Safety and professionalism (boundaries, mandatory reporting,
    > emergency procedures)

-   Specific curriculum content and teaching methods

**Background Checks and Safety:**

-   Criminal background checks through established vendors

-   Sex offender registry verification

-   Reference checks (2 professional references)

-   State-required mandatory reporter training (2-4 hours online)

-   Ongoing supervision requirements

-   Incident reporting and response protocols

### **The Volunteer-to-Employment Pipeline**

The volunteer program creates a talent pipeline for local coding
schools:

**How It Works:**

1.  Volunteers gain teaching experience during free community programs

2.  Their performance is evaluated by lead instructors and League staff

3.  Successful volunteers\' profiles (with permission) become visible to
    > partner schools

4.  Schools can search profiles when hiring and contact candidates
    > directly

5.  Schools conduct their own independent hiring process

6.  20-30 volunteers annually placed into employment across network

**Legal Framework:** Clear separation between volunteering and
employment:

-   Volunteering is genuinely voluntary with no promise of employment

-   Time limits prevent extended \"working interviews\" (3-6 months
    > maximum)

-   Schools make independent hiring decisions

-   Employment relationship is with school, not League

-   Profile system enables passive matching without obligations

**Value to All Parties:**

-   Volunteers: Gain teaching experience and employment opportunities

-   Schools: Pre-vetted candidates with demonstrated teaching ability

-   League: Cost-effective program delivery and partner satisfaction

-   Students: Consistent quality instruction from vetted educators

### **Volunteer Recognition and Retention**

**Milestone Recognition:**

-   5 events: Certificate, League t-shirt

-   10 events: Featured in newsletter, reference letter available

-   25 events: \"Master Volunteer\" status, priority scheduling

-   50 events: \"Elite Volunteer\" recognition, annual appreciation
    > event

**Ongoing Engagement:**

-   Monthly volunteer newsletter with teaching tips and success stories

-   Private volunteer community (Facebook group or Slack channel)

-   Quarterly meetups for training and social connection

-   Professional development workshops on advanced teaching techniques

-   Clear pathway to increased responsibility and impact

### **Budget and ROI**

**Annual Investment (per metro):**

-   Regional Volunteer Coordinator: \$52,500

-   Background checks and training: \$1,200

-   Recruitment marketing: \$2,000

-   Recognition and retention: \$1,500

-   **Total: \$57,200 per metro**

**Return on Investment:**

-   30 active volunteers × 20 hours annually = 600 volunteer hours per
    > metro

-   10 metros = 6,000 volunteer hours nationally per year

-   At \$30/hour equivalent value = \$180,000 in-kind contribution per
    > metro

-   \$1.8M total in-kind value across 10-metro network

-   **ROI: 3x direct financial value, plus talent pipeline and
    > sustainability benefits**

## **6. Regional Gear Libraries**

### **The Equipment Access Challenge**

Quality CS and robotics education requires equipment that many schools,
libraries, and community organizations cannot afford to purchase. A
single set of robotics kits for a class of 10 students can cost
\$2,000-5,000. Chromebooks or laptops suitable for programming run
\$200-400 each. Microcontroller kits (Arduino, Micro:bit, Raspberry Pi)
cost \$30-100 per student. For organizations wanting to offer occasional
programming events or short-term programs, purchasing equipment
represents an insurmountable barrier---the cost cannot be justified for
intermittent use.

Additionally, many schools have equipment sitting unused---robotics kits
from previous programs that ended, computers that were replaced but
still functional, or donated materials that never found a home. This
equipment has value but no mechanism for redistribution to organizations
that need it.

### **The Gear Library Solution**

Regional gear libraries solve both problems: they provide rotating
access to equipment that maximizes utilization while minimizing cost,
and they create a system for collecting, refurbishing, and
redistributing donated equipment that would otherwise sit idle.

**Core Functions:**

1.  **Equipment Collection and Refurbishment** - Accept donations of
    > used computers, robotics kits, and other technology from schools,
    > companies, and individuals. Partner organizations refurbish
    > donated computers (install operating systems, update software,
    > replace worn components) and inventory/test robotics equipment to
    > ensure functionality.

2.  **Lending Operations** - Make equipment available for loan to:

    -   Our own free Tech Club events and multi-week programs

    -   Partner coding schools delivering contracted programs

    -   Schools running short-term CS units or after-school programs

    -   Libraries hosting programming events

    -   Community organizations (scouts, homeschool co-ops, youth
        > groups) running clubs

    -   Teachers piloting CS education before purchasing their own
        > equipment

3.  **Kit Assembly and Management** - Assemble curriculum-specific kits
    > that contain everything needed for particular programs (e.g.,
    > \"Python Game Development Kit\" with 6 Chromebooks, mice, power
    > strips, and curriculum materials; \"VEX Robotics Kit\" with
    > robots, controllers, field elements, and tools). Maintain
    > inventory, track loans, coordinate delivery and pickup, and handle
    > repairs.

4.  **Technical Support** - Provide guidance on equipment use,
    > troubleshooting, and integration with curricula. Offer training
    > for teachers and volunteers on equipment setup and operation.

### **Partnership with Local Organizations**

Rather than operating gear libraries independently, we partner with
existing organizations that have relevant expertise and infrastructure:

**Computer Refurbishing Nonprofits:** Organizations like Computers 2 SD
Kids (San Diego), PCs for People (national), or regional equivalents
specialize in collecting donated computers, wiping and refurbishing
them, and distributing to underserved populations. They already have:

-   Warehouse or storage space for equipment

-   Technical staff who can refurbish and maintain computers

-   Relationships with corporate donors providing equipment

-   Established processes for accepting, processing, and distributing
    > technology

-   Insurance and liability coverage for equipment loans

We contract with these organizations to:

-   Host and manage our gear library equipment

-   Refurbish donated computers to our specifications

-   Track loans and maintain inventory systems

-   Coordinate delivery/pickup or serve as equipment checkout location

-   Receive \$3,000-5,000 annually in management fees for these services

**Robotics Competition Organizations:** FIRST Robotics Competition (FRC)
and FIRST Tech Challenge (FTC) programs operate through regional
nonprofits that support local teams. These organizations often have:

-   Storage space for robotics equipment and field elements

-   Technical expertise in robotics hardware and software

-   Relationships with schools and youth organizations doing robotics

-   Volunteer networks of engineers and technicians

-   Grant funding for STEM education equipment

We partner with these organizations to:

-   Store and maintain robotics kits (VEX, LEGO Mindstorms, etc.)

-   Assemble custom kits for specific programs

-   Provide technical support and training on robotics equipment

-   Connect schools piloting robotics to competition programs if
    > interest develops

-   Receive \$2,000-3,000 annually for equipment management

**Makerspaces and STEM Nonprofits:** Community makerspaces and STEM
education organizations may serve as alternative hosts, particularly for
specialized equipment (3D printers, laser cutters, advanced electronics)
that supplement core programming education.

### **Equipment Inventory (Typical Metro)**

**Computers:**

-   30-40 Chromebooks or refurbished laptops suitable for programming

-   Organized in sets of 6-8 for classroom use

-   All configured with development environments (Python, Java, web
    > browsers)

-   Regularly updated and maintained

**Robotics Kits:**

-   10-15 LEGO Mindstorms or VEX IQ kits (elementary/middle school
    > level)

-   5-8 VEX EDR or similar kits (middle/high school level)

-   20-30 Arduino or Micro:bit starter kits (individual student use)

-   8-10 Raspberry Pi kits with accessories

-   Assorted sensors, motors, and electronic components

**Support Equipment:**

-   Power strips, extension cords, cable organizers

-   Mice, keyboards, and peripherals

-   Carrying cases and storage containers

-   Charging stations for battery-powered devices

**Value:** Total equipment value per metro: \$25,000-40,000

### **Loan Procedures and Policies**

**Eligibility:** Organizations in our network (venues, delivery
partners, Tier 3 community club leaders) can borrow equipment after
completing brief orientation on proper use and return procedures.

**Loan Terms:**

-   Short-term (1-2 weeks): For one-off events or short programs

-   Medium-term (6-12 weeks): For semester programs or extended clubs

-   Long-term (academic year): For schools or organizations with
    > sustained programming

**Fees:**

-   Our own programs: Free equipment use

-   Partner organizations delivering contracted programs: Free or
    > nominal fee (\$25-50) for administrative costs

-   Schools and community organizations: \$50-100/month rental fee to
    > cover insurance, maintenance, and replacement costs

-   Tier 3 community clubs: \$50-75/month for equipment kits

**Damage and Loss:** Borrowing organizations sign agreements accepting
responsibility for lost or damaged equipment. Insurance covers
catastrophic loss (theft, fire). Normal wear and tear is expected and
covered by rental fees.

**Delivery and Pickup:** For large equipment loans (10+ devices),
partner organization may provide delivery and pickup. For smaller loans,
borrowers pick up and return to gear library location.

### **Revenue Model**

**Income Sources:**

-   Educational product company donations: Equipment provided at no cost

-   Equipment rental fees: \$3,000-6,000 annually per metro

-   School/organization equipment purchases: Partner hosts may sell
    > refurbished equipment to schools wanting permanent access

-   Corporate equipment donations: Tax deductions incentivize donations

**Expenses:**

-   Partner organization management fees: \$5,000-8,000 annually

-   Insurance on equipment: \$1,000-2,000 annually

-   Equipment repairs and replacement: \$2,000-4,000 annually

-   Transportation and logistics: \$1,000-2,000 annually

**Net Cost Per Metro:** \$6,000-10,000 annually (substantially offset by
rental income and equipment donations)

### **Strategic Benefits**

The gear library creates multiple advantages:

**For Programs:** Equipment access enables robotics and physical
computing programs that would be impossible without capital investment.
Programs can pilot new topics (Arduino, Raspberry Pi, specific robotics
platforms) without purchasing equipment first.

**For Venues:** Schools and libraries can host programming events
without equipment investment. Can evaluate student interest before
committing budget to purchases.

**For Educational Product Companies:** Gear library provides
distribution channel for product exposure. Students and teachers
experience their platforms in real programs, creating adoption pipeline.

**For Equipment Donors:** Schools and companies can donate unused
equipment that benefits students rather than ending in landfills. Tax
deductions for donations. Corporate donors demonstrate community impact.

**For Partner Organizations:** Computer refurbishing nonprofits and
robotics organizations receive steady revenue (\$5,000-8,000 annually)
for equipment management that leverages their existing expertise and
infrastructure.

**For Sustainability:** Equipment continues serving students even if
individual programs end or our funding fluctuates. Gear library
infrastructure outlasts specific program deployments.

### **Integration with Other Functions**

The gear library connects seamlessly with other operational components:

-   **National Partnerships:** Raspberry Pi Foundation and Grant Imahara
    > Foundation programs require specific equipment that gear libraries
    > provide

-   **Venue Development:** Equipment availability enables venues without
    > computers or robotics to host programs

-   **Delivery Partners:** Coding schools can deliver robotics programs
    > without owning expensive equipment

-   **Community Clubs:** Tier 3 club leaders can access equipment their
    > organizations couldn\'t purchase

-   **Educational Product Companies:** Partner vendors donate equipment
    > to gear libraries, gaining exposure and adoption

The gear library transforms equipment from a capital barrier into a
shared resource that amplifies program reach and sustainability.

## **Operational Integration and Scale**

### **How the Six Functions Work Together**

These six operational components function as an integrated system:

1.  **National partners** provide curriculum and contribute to
    > coordinator costs

2.  **Venues** provide space and promotion to their communities

3.  **Delivery partners** provide instruction supported by volunteers

4.  **Marketing infrastructure** fills programs with students

5.  **Volunteer system** reduces costs and builds talent pipeline

6.  **Gear libraries** provide equipment enabling programs that would
    > otherwise be cost-prohibitive

The result is a coordinating function that makes each component more
effective than it could be independently, while reducing total system
costs by 60-70% compared to each organization building complete
infrastructure.

### **Regional Coordinator Role**

The regional coordinator is the linchpin connecting all six functions:

-   Maintains relationships with national partners, venues, and delivery
    > partners

-   Coordinates scheduling and logistics across all programs

-   Manages volunteer recruitment, training, and deployment

-   Oversees marketing and registration activities

-   Ensures quality standards across all programs

-   Reports outcomes to funders and partners

-   Troubleshoots problems and maintains continuity

**Compensation:** \$45,000-60,000 annually (or \$25-35/hour for
part-time in smaller metros)

infrastructure

-   Validate operational model in new markets

-   Refine training materials and coordinator playbook

-   Establish initial national partnerships

-   Demonstrate proof of concept to funders

**Year 2:** Expand to 5 metros

-   Hire regional coordinators for new markets

-   Build venue networks in each metro

-   Recruit local delivery partners

-   Establish volunteer programs

-   Generate outcome data for funding proposals

**Year 3:** Scale to 10 metros

-   Continue geographic expansion

-   Strengthen national partnerships with increased volume

-   Develop coordinator network for shared learning

-   Implement advanced data systems and reporting

-   Achieve operational sustainability

**Years 4-5:** Reach 15-20 metros

-   National coverage including major markets

-   Mature operational systems and training

-   Strong evidence base for impact

-   Financial sustainability through diverse funding

-   Model ready for replication beyond initial organization
